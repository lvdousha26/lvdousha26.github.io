A repost of an excerpt from "Remembering the 47th IMO in 2006", written by the Taiwan IMO team leader. Below is the reposted content (with abridgements).

The 2006 IMO, location: Slovenia.

## 7/07 — Problem Review Day

The Hotel Riviera I stayed at and the neighboring Hotel Slovenija (where Teacher Zuo stayed) are chain hotels. Meals were at the former, while meetings and all general assembly affairs were at the latter. I reported to the office and immediately received the shortlist — only the problems, no solutions. The idea was to let team leaders try the problems first, so their difficulty assessment would be more accurate. The entire next day was free time for working on the problems, with solutions distributed in the evening. I did not open them on the spot, as there were quite a few accompanying items, and most importantly, the students' names must not be wrong.

Back in the room, my roommate was already fast asleep. I called him to dinner; he mumbled, turned over, and went back to sleep, so I let him be. After dinner, I was very tired and did not feel like working on problems. Even though my roommate was snoring and the live band at the outdoor cafe outside was terribly loud and not very good, I was so exhausted that I fell into a deep sleep.

I woke up not feeling well-rested, still a bit dizzy. I went to breakfast at ten to eight. After eating, I opened the shortlist and casually glanced through it.

Oh my god! The last three problems in Algebra were all inequalities. A5 was another symmetric ternary inequality, clearly submitted by that Korean again. A wave of stomach pain — please do not let this one be selected! Even more absurd: oh my god, there was not a single functional equation problem, not one! In Taiwan, we had reviewed functional equations intensively; what a disappointment. Then, oh my god! There were ten geometry problems, a record. Then I flipped to Combinatorics... Oh my god! The fourth combinatorics problem, C4, was one I submitted myself. Intense stomach pain. I thought this problem was around IMO problem 5 difficulty — hard to write and hard to grade — classified by the committee as "medium-hard." Behind it were C5, C6, C7. Finally, Number Theory, nothing particularly striking.

> Note: This year's IMO shortlist can be found in the book "International Mathematical Olympiad Shortlist Solutions" published by Zhejiang University Press.

The first Jury Meeting started at nine o'clock, but only a few announcements were made before adjourning. Today's task was for team leaders to work on the problems and give feedback. Solutions would be given to team leaders at 4:30 PM. This year, we would not meet the students at all before coordinating scores, which was really unfortunate. Fortunately, the students this year had decent表达能力 — I was only worried that Pixun might be too terse. Everything else should be fine, I hoped.

I spent the entire day thinking about these problems. A few of them I had no idea where to start. I predicted this year's problems would be harder than last year's, and the thought of the scores made me dizzy again. The committee was quite humane — internet access was available, but only near the lobby. The chairman specifically said in the morning that banning internet access was pointless; with technology so advanced, anyone determined to cheat would find a way, such as using a mobile phone or simply making a call on the roadside. Everyone appreciated the open internet access this year. I used Skype to call home; the audio was clear and real-time, but amusingly, the USB headset I brought did not work, so all sound came through the laptop speakers. Mom's voice echoed through the lobby, and we talked for ten minutes! Anyway, the lobby was noisy with many people talking, and they could not understand Taiwanese Hokkien. Plus, it was really nice to hear Dad's voice.

My progress on the problems was not smooth throughout the day. This year's shortlist was quite unbalanced. The combinatorics problems were all of a high standard, and the geometry was decent, but number theory and algebra were skewed — there was not a single problem I found beautiful. Throughout the day, comments and alternative solutions from various national team leaders kept appearing. Some were clearly the work of strong mathematicians, such as the leaders from Colombia and Romania.

Combinatorics problem C4 was one I submitted. It was originally based on a theorem from advanced mathematics, but the entire problem could be stated and solved using elementary methods. Early in the morning, the Colombian team leader commented that this was a known theorem in graduate-level combinatorics. I was not surprised, as he was a student of Stanley and would naturally be very familiar with it. But his comment that the key theorem was "well-known" is probably only well-known to people in combinatorics. Because of this comment, I thought C4 would probably not become an official problem.

After lunch, I took a good nap and fully recovered from the morning's fatigue. At 4:30 PM, I received the reference solutions. I glanced through them — several problems were extremely difficult. Even the official solutions filled two full A4 pages. What was that about? How could students possibly solve these? And it was not just one problem but four or five. I worried about how badly Taiwan's scores would turn out this year. Just thinking about it gave me a stomachache.

Comments kept coming. Around 6 PM, the South African team leader left a comment on C4, claiming that the problem had redundant conditions and was flawed. I looked carefully and found it unbelievable — he had not even understood the problem. This was intolerable. I wrote a "comment on the comment on C4" and submitted it to the committee, refuting his remark, then went back to my room to rest.

Back in my room, I had second thoughts. This was actually quite impolite; I could have handled it more diplomatically. For instance, I could have found him first and asked him to withdraw the comment privately, without publicly calling him wrong. In mathematics, things are black and white — right or wrong — but my comment was too targeted. Putting myself in his shoes, I would have felt very embarrassed — that is only human. Moreover, since I was the one who submitted the problem, this made it even worse. Whether or not it was selected as an official problem (though it seemed unlikely), everyone would eventually know it came from Taiwan. Looking at my refutation, it came across as petty.

The more I thought about it, the more uncomfortable I felt. So I rushed back to the office and asked to withdraw my comment. They were about to start printing, and they had already written on the South African comment: "See the Taiwan team leader's comment on this comment" — it was far too obvious. Fortunately, Andrej, the committee member in charge of problems, was there. I explained the situation — that my comment was too confrontational, rude, and might damage our rapport. He was very understanding and said, "I'll handle it." He immediately found the South African team leader, a courteous elderly gentleman. We discussed it briefly, and he finally realized he had misread the problem. He agreed with the负责人 to withdraw the comment. The matter was resolved amicably. I was in a great mood — the committee负责人 handled it responsibly, I handled it well, and the elderly gentleman was very reasonable.

In the evening, there was a banquet hosted by some prime minister. My roommate, the Macau team leader, along with the mainland China and Hong Kong team leaders, were all dressed very formally. I only wore a tie without a suit jacket, which was a bit embarrassing. But when we gathered, it turned out we were overdressed. Several people were just wearing jeans and T-shirts. We first walked to the neighboring five-star hotel, stood in the lobby drinking a cocktail, and listened to the prime minister's speech. Ten minutes later, we went upstairs to dinner. I was already a bit tipsy. The food was mediocre, but the service, atmosphere, and decor were genuinely nice. I finally tried sparkling mineral water for the first time. As Du Mao commented two years ago, it tasted like Sprite without sugar — indescribably odd.

The food was average; the only highlight was smoked beef with cantaloupe. I had discovered this classic dish last year when Masa, a Japanese friend I met while traveling in Italy, treated me to it. It was truly delicious. Looking around, it seemed no one at my table knew it was meant to be eaten together. I happily had two servings. Anyone reading this should try it —教官 strongly recommends smoked beef with cantaloupe. Do not doubt it; eat them together. Surprisingly delightful.

After eating and drinking, everyone opened up. The mainland China team leader (LSH) worked in financial mathematics, and the Macau team leader worked in algebraic geometry — both were actively doing research. We discussed the recently solved Poincare conjecture, talked about the state of olympiad mathematics in different places, and exchanged mathematical news. It was a pleasant dinner. Afterwards, we strolled through a seaside night market selling handicrafts, which were quite exquisite. The scenery was beautiful, with a cool gentle breeze — very comfortable. I will take some photos tomorrow evening.

Tomorrow we start voting to select the problems. I hope the results are not too extreme this year.

## 7/08 — Problem Selection Day

The problem selection meeting starting at 9 AM was very memorable. The selection committee sat right next to me — five well-known figures in the Olympiad community. First, the committee chairman Andrej (also the overall score coordinator, the same person who helped me find the South African team leader yesterday) gave a general overview of this year's problems. A total of 135 problems were submitted by various countries, and the 30 problems in our hands were what remained. So I should be pleased — making it to the shortlist is not easy.

Early in the morning, Teacher Zuo asked if I wanted to distribute our exchange problems along with the gifts. I said hold on, because one of our exchange problems (problem 3) was related to shortlist problem C2. Although the techniques for C2 were different, the figure was the same, which gave at least a small advantage. If we distributed the problems now and other team leaders saw our problem 3, C2 would not be selected. Teacher Zuo agreed that this made sense, so we held off on distributing exchange problems and gifts, deciding to play it by ear.

The entire process followed this pattern: the chairman would set a topic, everyone would express their opinions fully, then a proposal would be made, followed by a vote.

The selection officially began. The chairman asked if anyone had comments on any problem. First was N7 — someone pointed out it was a generalization of a known problem, so it was voted out immediately. I had a feeling my C4 would also be eliminated. Then came A5 — the Greek team leader said it might have been seen before, but could not provide evidence. Then my C4 — the Colombian team leader said it was a good problem, but a very small number of students might know it. The chairman said to set it aside for now. Then C3 — a team leader questioned its similarity to a just-distributed Iranian contest problem. The Iranian team leader admitted it was very similar, so it was voted out. You see — my morning concerns were justified.

Then a team leader suggested that N4 (which became this year's IMO problem 5) was actually very algebraic and should be moved to Algebra. Everyone was dissatisfied with this year's Algebra shortlist. The selection committee explained, and the chairman asked everyone to just note it as cross-domain. Then the four categories were opened for further comments. Algebra had no objections. Then came Combinatorics.

The German team leader again suggested removing C4, for the same reason — we cannot take the risk. The vote: 41 to 27 in favor of removal. Alas, my first problem to ever make it into an IMO shortlist was thus killed. I was a bit sad at the moment, but on second thought, it was for the best. If it had been selected, I would have had a real headache. This problem was truly hard to write and hard to grade. Do not forget — I would be the one coordinating scores. It would have been like digging a trap and jumping into it myself, just like the APMO 2005-4 firefighter problem I submitted two years ago — everyone complained bitterly, and not a single person in Taiwan solved it correctly. There was no need for that. Making it to the shortlist already made me happy, and indeed, some students might have known the advanced mathematics background.

Geometry had no objections either. Number theory had no major issues. Then came a discussion about whether to select hard problems first. After some opinions were exchanged, the chairman decided to select hard problems first, with a break until 11:15. At this point, it was clear that the chairman was subtly steering the agenda and selection preferences. I sighed — the atmosphere was that this year's problems "must" be harder. In reality, only a few team leaders spoke up; some were truly thoughtless. The strong team leaders carried weight in their words, while the weak ones just kept talking aimlessly.

During the break, something surprising happened. Andrej approached me and said that we had once been roommates in Greece! I thought about it — my god, it was true! Although only for a day, he had arrived late at night and left in the middle of the night, and during the day he was busy as an observer for the organizing committee, so we barely exchanged a few words. When he left, he had placed a commemorative T-shirt on my luggage.

During the break, everyone received a ballot. Among the problems that had not been eliminated, team leaders checked off each problem as Easy, Medium, or Hard, and as Trivial, Okay, or Beautiful. This would be an important basis for selection.

The meeting resumed. It was decided, following last year's format, to first group problems 3 and 6 together, then 1 and 4, and then consider 2 and 5 individually. The voting results were distributed, and the selection of problems 3 and 6 began. After proposals and voting, the remaining candidates were A6, C5, C6, C7, G8, and G10. Privately, I really did not want C5 or C6 to be selected, as they would be very hard to grade. A6 (an inequality that became this year's IMO problem 3) was also not easy, but when it comes to inequalities, either you solve it or you do not — there are no partial points to argue over — making it easier to grade. Given the situation of this year's Taiwan team, I thought the students might have a chance with this inequality. G8 was a difficult geometry problem, our strong suit — I strongly supported it. G10 was a combinatorial geometry problem (this year's IMO problem 6) that we had no chance of solving, but it seemed likely to be selected now. This problem had a slight connection to an exercise in the combinatorial geometry handout I had compiled earlier. However, no special class was held this year; the handout was just distributed to students. It also had a slight connection to a mock exam problem I had given to the team two years ago, but that material was not distributed this year. Both connections were only tenuous. So I was frustrated and had a stomachache.

After proposals, five candidate pairs emerged: (A6,G10), (G10,C5), (G10,C6), (C5,G8), (A6,C5). Voting began, with the lowest-voted pair eliminated each round. First (A6,C5) was eliminated, then (C5,G8), then (G10,C6) (I was quite regretful — C6 was a very beautiful problem). Throughout the process, the vote counts were very close, differing by only two or three votes. Two pairs remained: (A6,G10) and (C5,G10). So G10 was already through.

I had much to consider. I wanted C2 to be selected. I very much did not want A5, that ternary inequality, to be selected — clearly submitted by that Korean again. Also, the students had the wrong impression that such inequalities could be solved by brute force, but brute-forcing this one would be a disaster. If it were selected, it would be terrible. Taiwan students are not bad at algebra; although A6 was difficult, they should be able to make some progress. Compared to C5 — a combinatorics problem that is hard to solve, hard to guess the answer, and hard to coordinate scores for — it was really not a good choice. Finally, if C5 were selected, then C2 would be out. So I voted for (A6,G10).

The result: (A6,G10) won with 41 votes against (C5,G10)'s 39 votes. My god, that was intense. Clearly, although no one said it outright, we all knew that A6 would be problem 3 and G10 would be problem 6. Since C3 and C4 were already eliminated, the remaining combinatorics candidates were only C1 and C2 — the students should handle those fine. If the next round selected a moderately difficult geometry problem for positions 2 and 5, that would greatly benefit us.

> Note: A = Algebra, C = Combinatorics, G = Geometry, N = Number Theory.

It was already noon. The US team leader was very unimpressed with these two problems, especially A6. He said the team leaders were selecting problems they themselves could not solve, without actually trying them. Actually, he was right. I had tried A6 yesterday, and it was really not easy. I said that my original prediction was that either C5 or C6 would be selected — both were excellent problems. But neither was. As a combinatorics person, I was a bit disappointed. But on the bright side, it meant everyone would have to fight it out on problems 1, 2, 4, and 5. This echoed what I told the students before we left: this year's problems will definitely be hard, because the past two years were easy, and we were returning to Eastern Europe, the birthplace of the IMO. So do not dwell too much on problems 3 and 6; focus on doing 1, 2, 4, and 5 well. Now that problems 3 and 6 seemed hopeless, this was not necessarily a bad thing. It felt like a silver lining. From a score coordination perspective, the most troublesome problems (C5, C6) were no longer in play. Now I was praying for a difficult conventional geometry problem among problems 2 and 5.

In the afternoon, when selecting problems 1 and 4, the weaker countries that were just along for the ride had many opinions — mainly that students should not get zero scores. The final candidates were A1, C1, C2, G1, G2, N1, and N3. After discussion and voting, the following pairs emerged: (N1,A1), (A1,G1), (N1,G1), (A1,N3), (N3,G1), (N1,G2), (A1,G2). In my mind, the best outcome was to have C2 and a geometry problem as problems 2 and 5 — that would be most advantageous for Taiwan. So I had no choice but to vote for (N1,A1) and (A1,N3).

Things did not go as I wished. The weaker IMO countries very much wanted students to be able to score points, so they strongly favored selecting an easy geometry problem for problem 1. This was quite不利. In the first round, (A1,G1) and (A1,G2) were eliminated; in the second round, (A1,N3) was eliminated; in the third round, (N3,G1) was eliminated; in the fourth round, (N1,A1) was eliminated; in the fifth round, (N1,G2) was eliminated. This left (N1,G1). This outcome brought both joy and concern. The concern: our strength in geometry was gone, which was really frustrating. The joy: this meant C2 would almost certainly be selected.

Then came the selection of problems 2 and 5. By a vote of 35 to 34, it was decided to select a combinatorics problem first, then pair it with another problem. At this point, someone actually proposed C5, with quite a few supporters! Were they crazy? That would make this the hardest IMO in history. The Hungarian team leader, who was also the general assembly chairman, gave some timely反对意见. After votes, C5 and C1 were eliminated in turn, and C2 was successfully selected.

One problem remained. The meeting continued. The situation now was: problem 2 would be C2, and they needed to select a problem 5 with some algebraic flavor. The remaining A1 through A5 were unpopular with little choice, leaving only N3, N4, and N5 which still had some algebraic character. First, N5 was eliminated, leaving two. I thought N3 was doable for the students, but not substantial enough to be problem 5. I believe anyone who has seriously worked through IMO problems would feel the same. At this point, the room was clearly split into two camps: the IMO aesthetic balance camp and the scores-first camp.

I was very conflicted, and I believe everyone else was too. When N5 was just eliminated, N3 and N4 each had 40 votes. Now it was a binary choice — really, really hard to decide. The result: 41 to 41, a tie! The whole room was shocked; no one could make up their mind. The chairman called for full discussion. Finally, another vote: N4 won 45 to 37. Which side did I vote for? Not telling.

Everyone was exhausted, but they pushed through to decide the order as well. There was not much choice since problems 3 and 6 were already fixed. Out of four proposals, two were eliminated early (NNAGCG and NCAGNG). Then GCANNG beat GNANCG 41 to 37. And so, the six problems of this year's IMO were decided. I heard it was the first time in ten years that the selection was completed in a single day. I was not entirely satisfied, but I had to accept it.

## 7/09 — Translation Day and World Cup Day

It is now 11:20 PM. Outside, cars are honking wildly as they speed by — must be Italians. Half an hour ago, they won the World Cup, and I was watching, my blood boiling. Here in Europe, the 2006 World Cup, and I am in Europe.

I arrived at the venue at 9 AM only to find that it was the English version confirmation meeting. I listened for a while and found it incredibly boring — a bunch of people nitpicking over wording. At 11 AM, the meeting to confirm the English version of the exam problems began, followed by instructions on how to translate and upload files. Problem 2 was discussed endlessly, getting more confused with each revision. It was unbearable. The UK team leader was appointed临时 chairman and managed to fend off some nonsensical proposals. The English version was finally settled.

Andrej then explained the translation and file upload process. This year's system was quite standardized: use the provided template, insert your language's version of the problems, and upload the file. The format was mandatory, so the materials Taiwan brought were completely useless. Teacher Zuo, who was responsible for translating the problems, was completely unaware and was still outside working hard on the translation. I discovered that the committee's upload system would have a major issue: traditional and simplified Chinese files would be uploaded to the same location called "Chinese", and the later upload would overwrite the earlier one. I quickly found Andrej, and by noon the problem was resolved — Chinese was split into "Chinese (traditional)" and "Chinese (simplified)". Very clear and efficient.

But when I discussed it further with Andrej, I realized a bigger problem: each student could choose two language versions of the exam. Besides Taiwan using traditional Chinese and mainland China and Hong Kong/Macau using simplified Chinese, there were also over twenty Chinese-heritage students from more than a dozen countries who chose "Chinese". Now the question was: do these students use traditional or simplified? I said there was no way for me to know, so the staff had a new task — contacting the deputy leaders of those countries to reconfirm with the students.

At meals, the Chinese-speakers always gathered together. In fact, most Asians are similar — introverted by nature, and those who are not fluent in English do not initiate conversations. Westerners still look down on Asians. These past few days, I tried chatting with some unfamiliar team leaders. Some were quite friendly, others were hard to approach. Whatever. Near the end of the meal, the US team leader came over to chat; we had a pleasant conversation. The US team leader was ethnically Chinese and had a lot to say about the cultural differences between Chinese and Americans. The US is a highly competitive society that encourages striving, and it is precisely this competition and encouragement that makes it so strong and produces so many top talents. He was also quite dismissive of the nitpicking team leaders — since they could not solve problems, they made themselves useful by splitting hairs over wording. I laughed — that was spot on, great minds think alike.

I will be going to the US at the end of this month. Am I ready? For a society where you get left behind if you do not strive? Can I survive? I do not know myself. Yesterday, while walking on the sidewalk, someone mentioned that Tao would win the Fields Medal. My heart stirred again.

Translation continued in the afternoon. We switched to the committee's template, which took a long time. But we finally settled on a version. When it was time to upload, Teacher Zuo's computer suddenly could not connect to the internet, so I had to run back to get my computer. In any case, we uploaded a translation and breathed a sigh of relief. The office also confirmed that the Taiwan students had arrived safely.

It was already 5:40 PM, time for the APMO meeting. The APMO meeting was quite dull. Taiwan placed third this year, behind the US and South Korea, barely ahead of Japan. This was expected and not surprising. After the financial report, I asked whether the APMO could also compile yearbooks, including the full shortlist. This put the chairman on the spot. He said perhaps he could do it this year, but it would be hard to ensure continuation when the responsibility passed to others in subsequent years. The suggestion was ultimately shelved without further discussion. Next came the election of the host country for the next three years. With no one volunteering, South Korea successfully renewed.

At 7 PM, we finally went to dinner. Afterwards, I wanted to return to the office to confirm and sign the version I had uploaded in the afternoon. On the way, I ran into selection committee member Savchev. He looked very upset and spontaneously pulled me aside to complain (Savchev had visited Taiwan for problem selection when Taiwan hosted the IMO in 1998; he was an elder statesman in the math competition world). He said seeing me felt very familiar. We talked on the sidewalk for about fifteen minutes. The main issue was that after the official version of problem 6 was finalized, the mainland China and US team leaders both told him that students might misinterpret the symbol "b" at the edge as the side length. Then he asked me if I thought this was possible. I said, based on Chinese usage, it was "possible" that students would misunderstand the problem. His expression soured further. Because mainland China and the US are strong teams — if their students misinterpreted the problem, it would be serious. What irritated him even more was: they had meetings — why did no one speak up during the finalization meeting, only raising the issue after it was finalized? I comforted him and promised to carefully review the Taiwan version to ensure no student would misunderstand.

Hearing this made me nervous too. I quickly went back upstairs to compare the already-uploaded traditional Chinese version with the mainland Chinese simplified version. Upon comparison, I realized that mainland China did not follow the committee's wording and terminology at all — they completely translated it into their accustomed usage. Teacher Zuo and I had dutifully followed the original text, which actually constrained us and made our version sound very unnatural. In comparison, the mainland Chinese translation was much better. So we changed several problems to use the mainland version. Teacher Zuo was clearly tired and went back to rest. I uploaded, signed, and then went to the lobby to watch the World Cup final.

At that point, the score was 1:1. But I could not focus on the match. After sitting for ten minutes, I kept thinking about the problems and grew increasingly anxious. What if the students were genuinely misled? I went back to the computer room for a thorough re-check. I made several more corrections, uploaded a second version, checked it repeatedly, and finally signed.

Exhausted in both body and mind, I walked back to the hotel — the World Cup had ended, I was in Europe, and I missed it. How depressing. But heaven rewards the good. Just as I arrived at the entrance, I realized they were playing extra time! And it was just the start of the second half of extra time. About thirty people — team leaders and guests from various countries — had gathered around the outdoor TV wall, standing, sitting, or drinking beer. I quickly realized most were supporting Italy, probably because of proximity. So I watched fifteen minutes of a thrilling championship match, including the关键 Zidane headbutt to the chest and the red card. Unbelievable. And then it went to a penalty shootout. I was fortunate to witness it live, in Europe during the 2006 World Cup final, experiencing the Europeans' frenzy.

The moment France missed their second penalty kick, a kindly-looking elderly woman sitting next to me stood up and let out a scream that seemed inhuman. A team leader sitting not far in front of me was clearly drunk and extremely agitated, looking every bit a soccer hooligan. The Macau team leader, my roommate, sitting beside me, said: "This guy loves drinking, he's like this every year when drunk." The moment Italy secured victory, the whole crowd threw their arms up in celebration, and I jumped up excitedly too. Cheers erupted all around. I watched the synchronized waving of Italian flags in Rome, Milan, and Naples on the screen, and the Italian players and coaches embracing in ecstasy on the field. I was happy for them, but felt a bit lonely inside.

Back in the room, I chatted briefly with my roommate before we各自休息.

## 7/10 — Grading Standards Day

The meeting to discuss grading standards started at 11:00 AM. I thought I would have some time to properly understand the problems. I arrived at the venue at 9 AM, hoping to settle down and think through the problems, but the Singapore team leader said there were two versions of the traditional Chinese translation posted on the bulletin board. I was startled and went to find a staff member to correct it. Then, even more startling, around 10 AM, a staff member came to say they could not retrieve the file I uploaded yesterday. I rushed back to my room to get the file for them — another half hour gone. In the end, I only managed to roughly work through problem 5 before time ran out.

The grading standards meeting started with a bang. The Iranian team leader presented an elegant solution to problem 3 using Lagrange multipliers, turning it into a calculus problem with a very standardized approach. The UK team leader then proposed replacing the problem with N5 as problem 3. His argument: last year's problem 3, an inequality, could be solved using Muirhead's theorem, and the students had cursed at it online; now this year brings another problem solvable with calculus — was that not also inappropriate? He suggested N5 because it was short and could be translated quickly. In my heart, I agreed, because N5 involved Fermat's Little Theorem, which we were familiar with. But back then, no one even considered N5, and neither did I, since Fermat's Little Theorem was already tested last year — testing it again so soon would be unjustifiable. The chance of replacement was extremely slim.

The atmosphere was very serious. Selection committee member Kuzma came up to explain that Lagrange multipliers were indeed one valid method. The chairman skillfully prevented this议题 from becoming a formal proposal, and it eventually fizzled out. Andrej, Savchev, and Kuzma, who were sitting next to me (due to the巧妙 seating arrangement, I was right next to the five selection committee members), all let out long sighs. I could imagine the pressure they were under. If they actually replaced the problem, it would be a huge undertaking — all translations would have to be redone. That would be terrifying.

Next came the discussion of grading standards. The process was quite boring. The chief coordinator for each problem presented the standard, everyone had many opinions, and a finalized version would be proposed in the afternoon. And so it was noon. In the afternoon, discussions continued, and grading standards were finalized problem by problem. Particularly for problem 5: the first step was reducing to k=2, the second step was handling k=2. If a student directly stated that they had seen somewhere that this problem could be reduced to k=2, the originally allotted two points would become zero. This was a good thing — we should not encourage students to memorize random things.

During the meeting, I had more time to carefully examine several problems. I thought problems 1 and 4 were definitely fine; problems 2 and 5 should be okay if the students did not get nervous — the fixed-point technique in problem 5 was quite clever, but if they had absorbed the functional equations handouts properly, it should be manageable. Problem 2 was a constructive counting problem, which aligned with the combinatorics trend I had been emphasizing in recent years — parity analysis plus mathematical induction, also should be fine. For problem 3, students could get one point for factorization; beyond that, I had no idea how many students could solve it. Problem 6 would almost certainly be a complete failure for everyone. Looking at this, this year was really not looking good. If either problem 2 or 5 failed, we would be in trouble. But since problem 3 could be solved using Lagrange multipliers, perhaps Zheyun or Zhongheng, who had studied calculus, might have a chance. The other four would need to rely on their skill with elementary transformations. At this point, I predicted the best-case and worst-case scores: 159 and 125. We would see.

In the evening, we visited Piran, a small peninsula about five minutes away by car. ## 7/14 — Score Coordination Day 1

As team leader, together with Deputy Leader Teacher Hong, we also grade our own copy and arrive at a score. Then both sides sit down and compare. If they match, both sides sign and it is done. If they do not match, it becomes very troublesome. I have to work hard to explain the students' answers and fight for every point. English flies back and forth across the table; both sides give it their all — I try to push scores up, the other side tries to pull them down. Last year, I was the deputy leader, and I handled most of the coordination for Taiwan's difficult problems, so I was not nervous. The key to coordination is: do not lose your temper, be patient, show goodwill, do not be unreasonable, and take it slowly.

Bulgaria is a strong competition country. This year, the US and China both scored a perfect 42 on problem 2. So if Bulgaria scored more than 34 points, we would be overtaken. And that was very possible. Look at this situation: the general assembly was about to start, Bulgaria's score was still not out, and the completely uninvolved Taiwan team leader was getting nervous.

The general assembly was delayed by half an hour. Two countries appealed their coordination results to the assembly. The first: voted down, no points awarded. The second: Bulgaria — voted down, no points awarded. At that moment, I knew we had secured a top-10 finish. I was ecstatic.

Next came the determination of medal thresholds: 28 for gold, 19 for silver, 15 for bronze. And so, we ended up with one gold and five silvers. A perfect result.

The meeting ended at 11:30 PM. I was truly happy. Everyone in the delegation — teachers and students alike — knew that this team was slightly weaker than the previous two years. But with one gold, five silvers, and a 10th-place overall finish, we had still fulfilled our mission. Finally, I could sleep well.
